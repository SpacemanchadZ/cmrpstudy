Overview

How the Ramsey Multicraft Test Works

The Ramsey Multicraft Aptitude Test is a pre-employment assessment used by industrial employers to screen maintenance technician candidates. Here's what you need to know going in.

60–90
questions per form
Multiple-choice, 4 answer options. Time limit varies by employer (typically 30–45 min).
9
subject areas
Electrical, Motors, Mechanical, Hydraulics, Pneumatics, PLC/Controls, Print Reading, Math, Safety.
70%+
typical pass score
Most employers target 70–75%. Some set higher bars for senior roles. Know your employer's threshold.

What the Test Actually Measures

The Ramsey is not a theory test — it's an applied knowledge test. Every question is anchored to something a working multicraft tech actually does. The question style is generally: "given this situation, what happens" or "given these values, calculate this result."

💡 Key insight
You won't be asked to derive formulas. You will be asked to apply them. Memorize the formulas, understand what each variable means, and practice plugging numbers in.

How to Study Smart

1
Run through the flashcards once per domain — mark what you know vs. don't know. Don't try to memorize everything on pass 1.
2
Work through this study guide — read each domain's key rules and formulas. The SVG diagrams on the flashcard backs reinforce this.
3
Take the 91-question practice exam — score yourself. Use the domain breakdown to identify weak areas.
4
Drill weak domains — filter flashcards to just that domain. Re-read its guide section. Repeat the practice exam.
5
Use the calculators — work the formulas by hand on paper, then verify with the calculator. The test is pencil-and-paper, so practice without a calculator.
Domain 1

Electrical Fundamentals

Electrical questions test your understanding of basic circuit theory, wire identification, test instrument use, and protection devices. About 15% of the exam.

The Big Three Formulas

Ohm's Law
V = I × R
Cover any letter in the triangle to get its formula. V = volts, I = amps, R = ohms.
Power — 3 forms
P = V×I = I²R = V²/R
Use whichever form matches your known values. P = watts.
Series vs Parallel
RT = R1+R2 (series)
Parallel: 1/RT = 1/R1 + 1/R2. Same V across all branches.

Circuit Rules to Memorize

S
Series circuit: Current is the same everywhere. Voltage divides across resistors. One open = entire circuit dead. RT = R1 + R2 + R3.
P
Parallel circuit: Voltage is the same across all branches. Current divides. One open branch does NOT kill the others. 1/RT = 1/R1 + 1/R2.
!
Short circuit: Near-zero resistance path that bypasses the load. Causes massive current flow. Trips the breaker or blows the fuse.
G
Ground fault: Unintended current path from energized conductor to ground. GFCI trips at 5 mA imbalance in milliseconds.

US Wire Color Codes

ColorFunctionNotes
BlackHot (line) — 120V/240VAlways verify with meter
WhiteNeutralGrounded conductor
Green / bareEquipment groundSafety ground path
RedSecond hot (240V)Or traveler in 3-way
OrangeHigh-leg delta B phase208V to neutral — NOT 120V!
BlueC phase (3-phase NEC)Also used for travelers
⚠️ Critical rule
Wire colors can be mislabeled or re-used in older installations. Always verify with a meter before touching — even if the color looks right.

Test Instruments

Multimeter modes

  • VAC / VDC — Voltage. Power ON. Meter in parallel.
  • Amps (A) — Current. Power ON. Meter in series (or use clamp).
  • Ohms (Ω) — Resistance. Power OFF, component isolated.
  • Continuity — Power OFF. Beep = complete path. OL = open.

Megger (insulation test)

  • Applies 500–1000V DC to test winding insulation
  • Motor must be disconnected from line
  • >100 MΩ = excellent | 1–100 MΩ = acceptable | <1 MΩ = replace
  • Tests phase-to-ground AND phase-to-phase

Protection Devices

Fuse

One-time device. Melting element opens the circuit. Must be replaced after operation. Never upsize — always replace with same rating. Investigate WHY it blew first.

Circuit Breaker

Resettable device. Trips on overload or short circuit. Can be reset by hand. Provides same protection as fuse but is reusable. Check for tripped position (middle) before resetting.

💡 Exam tip
Grounding provides a low-resistance fault path so overcurrent devices trip before people are harmed. The ground wire is not meant to carry normal current — it only carries fault current.
Domain 2

Motor Systems

Motor questions cover 3-phase induction motors, motor nameplate data, protection devices, speed control, and single-phase motor types. About 15% of the exam.

3-Phase Induction Motor — How It Works

  1. Three-phase AC enters the stator windings, creating a rotating magnetic field.
  2. The rotating field induces current in the rotor, creating its own magnetic field.
  3. The rotor's field is attracted to the stator field — the rotor chases it and spins.
  4. The rotor always runs slightly slower than the field — this is called slip.
  5. No slip = no relative motion = no induced current = no torque. Some slip is required.

Key Formulas

Synchronous Speed
N = 120 × f / P
f = frequency (Hz), P = number of poles. 4-pole 60Hz = 1800 RPM.
Slip %
Slip = (Ns−Na)/Ns × 100
Ns = sync speed, Na = actual speed. Normal: 2–5% at full load.
Overload Setting
115–125% × FLA
FLA from nameplate. Set to protect windings from sustained overcurrent.

Motor Nameplate — What Each Field Means

FieldWhat it meansHow it's used
HPRated output horsepowerSize starter, conductors, overload
FLAFull load amps at rated HP/voltageBaseline for ALL protection settings
SFService factor — allowable overload multiplierSF 1.15 = 15% overload is OK
FRAMENEMA frame = mounting dimensionsEnsures bolt-in replacement
RPMFull load speed (actual, not sync)Calculate slip, gear/belt ratios
INS CLASSInsulation temperature ratingClass F = 155°C max winding temp
DUTYContinuous (CONT) or intermittentAffects thermal protection needs

Reversing a 3-Phase Motor

Swap any two of the three power leads (L1↔L2, L1↔L3, or L2↔L3). This reverses the rotating field direction and reverses the rotor. Always stop the motor completely before reversing — reversing under power causes massive current surge and mechanical shock.

Wye vs. Delta Connections

Wye (Y)
  • Windings share a common neutral point
  • Has a neutral conductor — can support 120V loads
  • Phase voltage = line voltage ÷ 1.732
  • Lower voltage per winding = lower insulation stress
Delta (Δ)
  • Windings form a closed triangle — no neutral
  • Phase voltage = line voltage
  • Higher circulating current capability
  • High-leg B phase = 208V to neutral (must be orange)

NEC Motor Protection Requirements

⚠️ NEC requires TWO separate protective devices
Branch circuit protection (fuse or breaker) at 175–250% FLA — handles inrush, protects wiring.
Overload relay at 115–125% FLA — protects the motor windings from sustained heat.

VFD (Variable Frequency Drive)

Converts fixed AC → DC bus → variable frequency AC. Speed is proportional to frequency. Provides soft start, energy savings at reduced loads (affinity laws: power ∝ speed³), and precise speed control. Most common application: pump and fan speed control.

Single-Phase Motor Capacitors

Start Capacitor

High value (100–800 µF). Short duty only — switched out by centrifugal switch at ~75% speed. Creates phase shift for extra starting torque. Will fail/explode if left in circuit.

Run Capacitor

Low value (2–70 µF). Stays in circuit continuously. Improves efficiency and power factor. Oval shape, smaller. If it fails, motor runs hot and may stall under load.

Domain 3

Mechanical Drives & Components

Mechanical questions cover power transmission, bearings, alignment, seals, and couplings. About 15% of the exam.

Gear & Belt Ratio Formulas

Gear Ratio
GR = Driven ÷ Driver
Output RPM = Input RPM ÷ GR. Output torque = Input torque × GR. Larger driven = slower, more torque.
Belt/Pulley Ratio
RPMout = RPMin × (Din ÷ Dout)
D = diameter. Larger driven pulley = slower output. Same ratio logic as gears.
Torque
T = F × d
Force × perpendicular distance. ft-lb or N-m. Longer wrench = more torque for same force.

Bearing Types

TypeHandlesBest for
Ball bearingRadial + axial loadsHigh speed, light-medium loads
Cylindrical rollerRadial onlyHeavy radial loads, moderate speed
Tapered rollerRadial + axial (combined)Gearboxes, wheel hubs, high combo loads
Thrust bearingAxial onlyVertical shafts, axial positioning
Journal (sleeve)Radial only (on oil film)Large slow shafts, turbines, compressors

Shaft Alignment

Parallel misalignment

Centerlines are parallel but offset. Causes 2× RPM radial vibration. Correct with shims under motor feet.

Angular misalignment

Centerlines meet at an angle. Causes 1× and 2× RPM axial vibration. Correct by shimming one side of motor.

Soft foot

One or more machine feet don't contact the baseplate flatly. Must correct BEFORE aligning. Tightening a soft foot bolt warps the frame and shifts the shaft.

Correct order

  1. Correct soft foot (shim the gap)
  2. Rough align by eye
  3. Precision align (laser or dial indicator)
  4. Tighten anchor bolts
💡 Alignment rule of thumb
A flexible coupling accommodates minor misalignment and vibration. It is not a substitute for alignment — proper alignment still required. The coupling just prevents premature failure from small residual errors.

Belt & Chain Drives

V-Belt tension
  • Under-tension: slipping, squealing, belt burns
  • Over-tension: bearing and shaft wear
  • Check deflection with tension gauge per manufacturer
  • Sheave grooves must be clean and undamaged
Chain drive rules
  • Correct sag = 1–2% of center distance
  • Lubricate per manufacturer specification
  • Never mix old and new chain on the same drive
  • Check sprocket teeth for hooked or worn profile
Domain 4

Hydraulics

Hydraulic questions test Pascal's Law, cylinder force, schematic reading, valve function, and pump operation. About 12% of the exam.

Foundation Principle: Pascal's Law

Pressure applied to a confined incompressible fluid transmits equally in all directions.

This is why a small input force on a small piston can produce a massive output force from a large piston. F = P × A. Hydraulics is force multiplication using fluid pressure.

Key Formulas

Pressure
P = F ÷ A
P = PSI, F = force in lbs, A = area in in². Rearrange for any unknown.
Cylinder extend force
F = P × (π × r²)
Full bore area for extend. Rod reduces area on retract side.
Pressure / Head
1 PSI = 2.31 ft head
Head = PSI × 2.31. PSI = Head ÷ 2.31.

System Components

ComponentFunctionKey rule
Relief valveLimits maximum system pressureOpens to tank when set pressure exceeded. Last line of defense.
Check valveAllows flow in one direction onlyBall/poppet seats against reverse pressure. Free flow forward.
Directional valveRoutes flow to extend/retract/hold4/3 = 4 ports, 3 positions. Rated in GPM.
Flow control valveRestricts flow to control speedMeter-out (exhaust) preferred over meter-in (supply).
AccumulatorStores hydraulic energy (nitrogen charged)DEPRESSURIZE before any maintenance. It is a pressure vessel.

Meter-Out vs. Meter-In

✓ Meter-out (preferred)

Flow control valve on the exhaust side of the cylinder. Creates backpressure that makes motion smooth and consistent under any load. Standard for most applications.

✗ Meter-in (avoid)

Flow control on the supply side. Cylinder can stall or lurch when an external load pushes back because there's no opposing pressure. Erratic motion under varying loads.

Contamination — #1 Cause of Hydraulic Failures

ContaminantEffectIndicator
Particles / wear debrisScored bores, worn pump gears, clogged orificesIncreased heat, erratic operation
WaterCorrosion, additive breakdown, fluid emulsificationMilky/cloudy fluid
AirCavitation, foamy fluid, spongy responseNoisy pump, frothy fluid
Heat / oxidationVarnish buildup, sludge, additive breakdownDark/burnt fluid smell
💡 Pump cavitation rule
Cavitation happens at the pump inlet — not the outlet. The inlet is the low-pressure side. Causes: clogged strainer, collapsed hose, fluid too cold/thick. Symptom: whining/grinding noise, low erratic pressure, rapid wear.
Domain 5

Pneumatics

Pneumatic questions cover FRL units, cylinder types, flow control, and compressed air system basics. About 10% of the exam.

FRL Unit — Always in This Order

Filter → Regulator → Lubricator (inlet to outlet, every time)

Filter removes water and particles first. Regulator sets pressure second. Lubricator adds oil mist last — for downstream components only. Some systems omit the lubricator for instrument air or clean-room applications.

Cylinder Types

Double-acting cylinder
  • Air powers both extend AND retract strokes
  • Requires 4-way directional valve
  • Controlled force in both directions
  • Standard for most industrial automation
Single-acting cylinder
  • Air extends only — spring retracts
  • Simpler, needs only 3-way valve
  • Spring limits retraction force and stroke
  • Used for clamping, fail-safe applications

Pneumatics vs. Hydraulics — Quick Comparison

FeaturePneumatics (air)Hydraulics (oil)
Typical pressure60–150 PSI500–3,000 PSI
Force capabilityLight to mediumVery high
Fluid typeCompressible (air)Incompressible (oil)
Position holdingPoor (air compresses)Excellent
SpeedFastModerate
CleanlinessClean (exhaust to air)Messy if leak

Air Leak Cost

Compressed air is the most expensive utility in most plants — yet 20–30% is typically wasted through leaks. A single 1/8-inch leak at 100 PSI wastes ~25 CFM continuously. The rule: set minimum pressure needed, fix leaks immediately.

💡 Regulator rule
A regulator can only REDUCE pressure — it cannot boost above supply. If you need 120 PSI but supply is only 100 PSI, you need a higher-pressure supply, not a bigger regulator.
Domain 6

PLC & Controls

PLC questions test ladder logic, I/O concepts, timers, counters, and basic troubleshooting. About 12% of the exam.

The PLC Scan Cycle

1
Read inputs — PLC reads all physical input states into the input image table.
2
Execute ladder logic — PLC solves every rung using the input image, updating the output image table.
3
Update outputs — PLC writes output image table values to physical output modules.
4
Housekeeping — communications, diagnostics. Typical cycle time: 1–20 ms. Repeat forever.

Ladder Logic Symbols

SymbolNameBehaviorAllen-Bradley
] [NO contactGap symbol. Passes power when bit = 1XIC
]/[NC contactSlash symbol. Passes power when bit = 0XIO
( )Output coilCircle. Bit = 1 when rung is trueOTE
[TON]Timer On DelayDone bit ON after preset delay. Resets if rung false.TON
[TOF]Timer Off DelayOutput ON immediately, stays ON for preset after rung false.TOF

Allen-Bradley I/O Addressing

Format: File : Word / Bit

I:1/3 = Input file, word 1, bit 3 (physical input terminal)
O:0/1 = Output file, word 0, bit 1
B3:0/5 = Bit file 3, word 0, bit 5 (internal bit)
T4:0.ACC = Timer file 4, timer 0, accumulated value
T4:0.DN = Timer file 4, timer 0, done bit

Sourcing vs. Sinking I/O

Sourcing (PNP)

Output supplies +24V. Current flows OUT of the output terminal. Common in European equipment. PNP sensor required.

Sinking (NPN)

Output provides a path to 0V. Current flows INTO the output terminal. Common in North American equipment. NPN sensor required.

⚠️ Force function warning
The Force function overrides physical I/O state — it bypasses safety interlocks. An output can energize even if limit switches are open. Always remove forces after troubleshooting. Never leave forced outputs in production.
Domain 7

Print Reading

Print reading questions test orthographic views, tolerances, symbols, and schematic interpretation. About 8% of the exam.

Orthographic Projection

Third-angle (US standard)

Views are placed in the direction you look to see them. Top view goes above the front view. Right side view goes to the right. Look for the truncated cone symbol — narrow end toward viewer.

First-angle (European)

Views are placed opposite the direction you look. Top view goes below the front view. Right side view goes to the left. Symbol shows wide end of cone toward viewer.

Line Types

Line typeAppearanceMeaning
Visible lineSolid, thickEdges or surfaces you can see
Hidden lineDashedEdges behind the current view
Center lineLong-short-long dashAxis of symmetry, hole centers
Section lineCrosshatch (diagonal lines)Cut surface in a section view
Cutting planeHeavy dash-dot with arrowsWhere the section cut is made

Tolerances

Bilateral tolerance: 2.500 ± 0.005 → acceptable from 2.495 to 2.505

Unilateral tolerance: 2.500 +0.010 / −0.000 → acceptable from 2.500 to 2.510

Rule: Any dimension within the tolerance range is acceptable — the nominal dimension is not required.

Title Block — Always Check First

  • Revision letter — Work only from the latest rev. Never use an older rev without authorization.
  • Scale — Never measure directly off a print. Use the dimensions shown.
  • General tolerance — Applies to all dimensions without individual tolerances (e.g., ±0.010 for 3-place decimals).
  • Drawing number — Unique identifier. Always reference this when ordering parts or filing paperwork.

Hydraulic & Electrical Schematic Symbols

Hydraulic

  • Circle with triangle OUT = pump
  • Circle with triangle IN = hydraulic motor
  • Solid line = working pressure
  • Long dashes = pilot/control signal
  • Short dashes = drain line
  • Dot at intersection = connected lines

Electrical / Ladder

  • ] [ gap symbol = NO contact
  • ]/[ slash = NC contact
  • ( ) circle = output coil
  • Vertical rails = L1 (hot) and L2 (neutral)
  • Reads left to right, top to bottom
Domain 8

Applied Math

Math questions test your ability to apply formulas with real numbers — no derivation required. About 8% of the exam. The calculator tab has these all built in.

Formulas You Must Know Cold

Area of a circle
A = π × r²
r = radius (half the diameter). Used for piston area, pipe cross-section, cylinder volume.
Cylinder volume
V = π × r² × L
1 gallon = 231 in³. Divide volume in in³ by 231 for gallons.
Surface speed
FPM = π × D × RPM ÷ 12
D in inches, divide by 12 to convert to feet. Grinding wheels, saws, rollers.
Pythagorean theorem
a² + b² = c²
3-4-5 right triangle used to check squareness. c = hypotenuse (longest side).

Domain 9

Safety & LOTO

Safety questions test OSHA standards, LOTO procedures, hazard identification, and PPE. About 5% of the exam — but 100% of staying alive.

LOTO — The 6 Steps (+ Verify)

1
Notify all affected employees that the equipment will be shut down.
2
Identify ALL energy sources — electrical, mechanical, hydraulic, pneumatic, thermal, gravitational.
3
Shut down the equipment using the normal stopping procedure.
4
Isolate every energy source at its isolation point (disconnect, valve, etc.).
5
Apply LOTO devices — your personal lock and tag on every isolation point.
6
Release stored energy — bleed hydraulic/pneumatic pressure, block elevated loads, discharge capacitors.
✓
VERIFY zero energy — attempt to start (push the START button), test with meter, confirm zero on all sources.
⚠️ LOTO cardinal rule
ONLY the authorized employee who applied the lock removes it. No exceptions — not for a supervisor request, not for end of shift, not for production pressure. Your lock = your life.

Hierarchy of Controls

Elimination
Substitution
Engineering controls
Administrative controls
PPE — last resort

PPE is the least effective control — it doesn't eliminate the hazard, it just protects the worker if the hazard reaches them. Always try higher-level controls first.

💡 Arc flash rule
Arc flash PPE category is based on incident energy (cal/cm²) — not voltage. A 480V bus can have higher incident energy than a 4160V bus depending on fault current and clearing time. Always check the arc flash label before working on energized equipment.
